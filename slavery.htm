Introduction

These web pages were designed to support “Slavery in the Classical World” (UCL course Hist. 7104).  However, I am not teaching the course this year, so the pages are “resting” and most material has been removed.  The one-term course is being run twice, October to December 2007 and January to March 2008, in the Department of History at UCL, taught by Dr Daniel Stewart.  Those interested should visit the History Department’s web-pages.

 

Useful Links

The Institute for the Study of Slavery (ISOS)

The Institute holds themed conferences on slavery each year, the proceedings of which are later published.  In 2001, it superseded the International Centre for the History of Slavery (ICHOS), originally founded in 1998 by the late Thomas Wiedemann (1950-2001).  The first conference was held in 2000, and six have been held so far:

The Body of the Slave (2000) [publ. as T. Wiedemann & J. Gardner (eds.), Representing the Body of the Slave (London, 2002) = Slavery and Abolition 23.2 (2002)]

Manumission (2001) [publ. as M. Kleijwegt (ed.), The Faces of Freedom: The Manumission and Emancipation of Slaves in Old World and New World Slavery (The Atlantic World 7; Leiden, 2006)]

5000 Years of Slavery (2002)

Resistance and Accomodation: Strategies of Survival in Slave Societies (2003).

Practices and Discourses of Abolition (2004)

Slavery, Citizenship and the State (2006)

Slaves, Cults and Religions (planned for 2008)

 

Greek Manumissions Project

This project aims to create a database of all Greek manumission inscriptions.  The current on-line material provides browsable versions of Boeotian inscriptions, with English translations, from the Hellenistic and Roman Periods.  The search function does not currently work.

 

Repertorium Familiae Caesaris

This is a prosopography of imperial slaves and freedmen compiled by the late Paul Weaver (d. 2005), whose classic work on the Familia Caesaris was published in 1972.  Although incomplete at his death, the still substantial and extensive repetorium has been prepared for publication by Professor Werner Eck of Cologne.

 

Slavery and Emancipation in the American South [Hist. 6101]

This course has not been run at UCL since 2002, but the old course-pages still provide a useful starting point for this topic, suggesting some basic books to read (and even films to see!).
