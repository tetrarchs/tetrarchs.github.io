Course plan

Week 1:           Introduction, historiography and ancient sources

Week 2:           Ancient ideas about of slavery: Aristotle, Seneca

Week 3:           Becoming a slave, sources of slaves, and demography

Week 4:           Slaves at work, agricultural, industrial, domestic

Week 5:           Treatment of slaves; torture

Week 6:           Slave revolts and other responses to slavery

Week 7:           Manumission and freedmen at Athens: manumission at Rome

Week 8:           Freedmen at Rome, social mobility, imperial civil service

Week 9:           ‘Unfree’ labour: Helots; eunuchs

Week 10:         Christianity and slavery; Paul, Augustine, late imperial legislation

 

COURSE-BOOK: T.E.J. Wiedemann, Greek and Roman Slavery (1981).  You should buy a copy of this book, as it will be required for every class.  Waterstone’s has been instructed to stock sufficient copies.

Please: 

1) include essay title, even if you devise your own additional title;

2) paginate your essays;

3) give word count (excluding bibliography);

4) spell check your essays and read through for sense; 

5) no excuses for misspelling ancient names or modern authors, esp. Wiedemann;

6) be consistent in style for ancient references and modern bibliography

 

 

1A       How crucial is the dichotomy slave/free in ancient thought?

or

1B        ‘The slave’s most lasting characteristic . . . was the fact that the mass of the slaves always belonged to the lower classes of society’ (Y. Thébert 1993).  Does this represent a fair description of the social realities of antique slavery?

 

2          Were EITHER the Stoics OR the Christians effective critics of slavery?

 

3          What part has demography to play in understanding slave supply and sources in antiquity?

 

4          How and why did Roman law regulate the treatment of slaves and how far, if at all, did this ameliorate the condition of slaves under the empire?

 

5A       How vital was slave labour in agriculture and industry for the domestic prosperity and overseas power of classical Athens?

or

5B        Do agricultural writers present an accurate picture of the role of slaves in the agriculture of Roman Italy?

or

5C       What part did slaves and their peculia play in the commerce of ancient Rome?

 

6          Why were slave revolts relatively uncommon in the ancient world? What were the chief factors driving those that did occur?

 

7A       How easy was it for a slave to gain freedom in ancient Rome and what factors motivated its being granted?

or

7B        To what extent did their former status constrain the activity or hinder the social mobility of freed slaves in Athens and/or Rome?

 

8          Were the Helots an asset or a hindrance to Sparta’s success at home and abroad?

 

9          What functions are served by slaves and slave status in ancient drama and poetry?  Discuss with particular reference to EITHER Aristophanes OR Plautus OR Augustan poetry?

Course Notes

Week One

Introduction

Definitions of slavery, modern historiography of slavery, ancient sources for slavery

Week Two

Ancient ideas of slavery

Aristotle, Seneca; how do you distinguish slave from free?

Week Three

Becoming a slave

Sources of slaves; the slave trade; demography and slavery; postliminium (Jerome, Life of Malchus)

Week Four

Slaves at work

Slaves in agriculture, commerce, industry and domestic service; the peculium in Roman law

Week Five

Treatment of slaves

Judicial torture at Athens and Rome; legal protection for slaves; family life for slaves

Week Six

Slave revolts and other slave responses to slavery

Roman slave revolts (Sicily, Spartacus); New World comparisons; other means of slaves ‘coping’ with slavery

Week Seven

Manumission and freedmen

Manumission and freedmen at Athens; social status at Athens (Demosthenes, Against Neaira); manumission at Rome

Week Eight

Freedmen at Rome

Freedmen and their social restrictions and mobility, careers; Petronius, Cena Trimalchionis; imperial freedmen and the civil service

Week Nine

Other forms of ‘unfree’ labour

Debt-bondage; the Helots at Sparta; eunuchs in the late empire

Week Ten

Slavery and Christianity

Paul; Augustine; Christianity and late Roman legislation
